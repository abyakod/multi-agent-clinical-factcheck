WHO ESSENTIAL MEDICINES — CLINICAL REFERENCE GUIDE
Source: WHO Model List of Essential Medicines, 23rd Edition (2023)
Classification: Public Domain

SECTION 1: CARDIOVASCULAR MEDICINES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

WARFARIN (Anticoagulant)
Indications: Prevention and treatment of thromboembolic disorders,
atrial fibrillation, mechanical heart valves, deep vein thrombosis,
pulmonary embolism.
Standard Dose: 2–10 mg/day orally, adjusted by INR monitoring.
Target INR: 2.0–3.0 for most indications; 2.5–3.5 for mechanical
heart valves.
Contraindications: Active bleeding, severe hepatic impairment,
pregnancy (first and third trimester), uncontrolled hypertension,
recent neurosurgery or ophthalmologic surgery.
Critical Drug Interactions:
  - NSAIDs (including ibuprofen, naproxen, aspirin): SEVERELY
    INCREASES bleeding risk. Concurrent use contraindicated unless
    benefit clearly outweighs risk. NSAIDs inhibit platelet function
    AND can increase warfarin plasma levels, leading to supratherapeutic
    INR and life-threatening haemorrhage.
  - Amiodarone: Significantly potentiates warfarin effect.
    Reduce warfarin dose by 30–50% and monitor INR closely.
  - Antibiotics (metronidazole, ciprofloxacin, fluconazole):
    Inhibit warfarin metabolism — increase INR monitoring frequency.
  - Vitamin K-rich foods (spinach, kale, broccoli): Reduce warfarin
    effect — advise consistent dietary intake.
Monitoring: INR every 1–4 weeks once stable. More frequent if dose
change, illness, or new medication introduced.
WHO Essential Medicine: Yes — List since 1977.

ATENOLOL (Beta-Blocker)
Indications: Hypertension, angina pectoris, acute myocardial
infarction, cardiac arrhythmias, heart failure (with caution).
Standard Dose: Hypertension: 25–100 mg once daily. Angina: 50–100 mg
once daily. Maximum 200 mg/day.
Contraindications: Cardiogenic shock, uncontrolled heart failure,
second or third degree AV block, severe bradycardia (HR < 45 bpm),
sick sinus syndrome, severe peripheral arterial disease, untreated
phaeochromocytoma.
Critical Drug Interactions:
  - Calcium channel blockers (verapamil, diltiazem): Additive
    negative chronotropic and inotropic effects — risk of severe
    bradycardia, AV block, and cardiac arrest. Avoid concurrent IV use.
  - Clonidine: Abrupt withdrawal of clonidine during beta-blocker
    therapy can cause severe rebound hypertension.
  - Insulin and oral hypoglycaemics: Beta-blockers mask tachycardia
    (a key hypoglycaemia warning sign). Use with extreme caution
    in insulin-dependent diabetics.
Special Populations: Reduce dose in renal impairment (CrCl < 35
mL/min). Use with caution in asthma — risk of bronchospasm.
WHO Essential Medicine: Yes.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

AMLODIPINE (Calcium Channel Blocker)
Indications: Hypertension, chronic stable angina, vasospastic angina.
Standard Dose: 5–10 mg once daily. Start at 2.5 mg in elderly or
hepatic impairment.
Contraindications: Cardiogenic shock, unstable angina (except
vasospastic), haemodynamically significant aortic stenosis.
Critical Drug Interactions:
  - Simvastatin: Amlodipine increases simvastatin plasma levels
    significantly. Do not exceed simvastatin 20 mg/day when combined
    with amlodipine — risk of myopathy and rhabdomyolysis.
  - Cyclosporin: Amlodipine increases cyclosporin levels —
    monitor levels and adjust dose.
  - CYP3A4 inhibitors (ketoconazole, ritonavir): Increase
    amlodipine plasma concentration — monitor for hypotension.
WHO Essential Medicine: Yes — added 2019.

SECTION 2: ANALGESICS AND ANTI-INFLAMMATORY MEDICINES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

IBUPROFEN (NSAID — Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drug)
Indications: Mild to moderate pain, fever, inflammatory conditions
(rheumatoid arthritis, osteoarthritis), dysmenorrhoea.
Standard Dose:
  Adults: 200–400 mg orally every 4–6 hours. Maximum 1200 mg/day
  (OTC); up to 3200 mg/day under medical supervision.
  Children (6 months–12 years): 5–10 mg/kg every 6–8 hours.
  Maximum 40 mg/kg/day.
Contraindications:
  - Active or history of peptic ulcer disease or GI bleeding.
  - Severe renal impairment (eGFR < 30 mL/min).
  - Severe hepatic impairment.
  - Third trimester of pregnancy — premature closure of ductus
    arteriosus and foetal renal dysfunction.
  - Concurrent anticoagulant therapy (ABSOLUTE CONTRAINDICATION
    with warfarin — see warfarin entry).
  - History of NSAID-induced asthma, urticaria, or rhinitis.
Critical Drug Interactions:
  - Warfarin: ABSOLUTE — concurrent use causes severely elevated
    bleeding risk through dual mechanism: platelet inhibition
    and increased warfarin bioavailability. Documented cases of
    fatal gastrointestinal haemorrhage.
  - ACE Inhibitors / ARBs: NSAIDs reduce antihypertensive effect
    and increase risk of acute kidney injury — the "triple whammy"
    combination (NSAID + ACEi/ARB + diuretic) is associated with
    acute renal failure.
  - Lithium: NSAIDs increase lithium levels by reducing renal
    clearance — risk of lithium toxicity.
  - Methotrexate: NSAIDs reduce renal methotrexate clearance —
    risk of methotrexate toxicity and bone marrow suppression.
WHO Essential Medicine: Yes.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

MORPHINE (Opioid Analgesic)
Indications: Severe pain — cancer pain, post-surgical, acute MI,
palliative care. Acute pulmonary oedema.
Standard Dose:
  Oral immediate-release: 5–15 mg every 4 hours.
  IV/SC: 2.5–5 mg every 4 hours. Titrate to effect.
  Modified-release oral: 12-hourly dosing once stable daily
  requirement established.
Contraindications: Acute respiratory depression, raised intracranial
pressure (with head injury), paralytic ileus, acute abdomen (before
diagnosis), MAO inhibitor use within 14 days.
Critical Drug Interactions:
  - MAO inhibitors (phenelzine, tranylcypromine, selegiline):
    LIFE-THREATENING serotonin syndrome and CNS excitation.
    Minimum 14-day washout required.
  - Benzodiazepines (diazepam, lorazepam, alprazolam): Additive
    CNS and respiratory depression. FDA Black Box Warning — combined
    use associated with respiratory arrest and death.
  - Alcohol: Additive CNS depression and respiratory depression.
  - Naltrexone / Naloxone: Precipitates acute withdrawal in
    opioid-dependent patients.
Special Populations: Reduce dose by 50% in renal impairment —
active metabolite (morphine-6-glucuronide) accumulates.
WHO Essential Medicine: Yes — core analgesic since 1977.

SECTION 3: DIABETES MEDICINES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

METFORMIN (Biguanide — First-line Type 2 Diabetes)
Indications: Type 2 diabetes mellitus — first-line pharmacotherapy.
Prevention of Type 2 diabetes in high-risk patients.
Standard Dose:
  Initial: 500 mg twice daily with meals, or 850 mg once daily.
  Titration: Increase by 500 mg weekly to minimise GI side effects.
  Maintenance: 1500–2000 mg/day in divided doses.
  Maximum: 3000 mg/day (limited by GI tolerance, not efficacy).
Contraindications:
  - eGFR < 30 mL/min — risk of lactic acidosis.
  - eGFR 30–45 mL/min — use with caution, reduce dose.
  - Acute or chronic metabolic acidosis, including diabetic
    ketoacidosis.
  - Severe hepatic impairment — lactic acidosis risk.
  - Iodinated contrast media — hold metformin 48 hours before
    and after IV contrast in patients with eGFR < 60 mL/min.
  - Acute conditions causing dehydration or tissue hypoxia
    (sepsis, severe infection, acute MI).
Critical Drug Interactions:
  - Alcohol: Increases lactic acidosis risk — advise against
    regular heavy alcohol consumption.
  - Topiramate, zonisamide: May increase metformin levels.
  - Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors: Increase lactic acidosis risk.
Monitoring: eGFR at baseline and annually. Vitamin B12 levels
every 2–3 years — metformin reduces B12 absorption.
WHO Essential Medicine: Yes.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

INSULIN (Regular Human Insulin)
Indications: Type 1 diabetes mellitus (mandatory). Type 2 diabetes
when oral agents insufficient. Diabetic ketoacidosis. Hyperosmolar
hyperglycaemic state. Hyperkalaemia (with glucose).
Standard Dose: Highly individualised — based on blood glucose
monitoring, carbohydrate intake, activity, illness.
  Typical starting dose (Type 2): 0.1–0.2 units/kg/day.
  DKA protocol: 0.1 units/kg/hour IV infusion.
Critical Drug Interactions:
  - Beta-blockers (atenolol, metoprolol, propranolol): Mask
    tachycardia warning sign of hypoglycaemia. Non-selective
    beta-blockers also impair glycogenolysis — prolonging
    hypoglycaemia. Use with extreme caution.
  - Corticosteroids: Significantly raise blood glucose — insulin
    dose increase required during steroid therapy.
  - Alcohol: Potentiates hypoglycaemic effect — patients must not
    skip meals when drinking alcohol.
  - Thiazolidinediones (pioglitazone): Additive glucose-lowering —
    hypoglycaemia risk, weight gain, fluid retention.
Storage: Unopened vials: refrigerate 2–8°C. Once opened: room
temperature (< 25°C) for up to 28 days. Never freeze.
WHO Essential Medicine: Yes — since original 1977 list.

SECTION 4: ANTIBIOTICS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

AMOXICILLIN (Aminopenicillin)
Indications: Community-acquired pneumonia, otitis media, sinusitis,
urinary tract infections, H. pylori eradication (in combination),
Lyme disease, dental infections.
Standard Dose:
  Adults: 250–500 mg three times daily, or 875 mg twice daily.
  Severe infections: 1 g three times daily.
  Children: 25–50 mg/kg/day in divided doses.
  Renal impairment (CrCl < 30): Increase dosing interval.
Contraindications: Penicillin hypersensitivity. Cross-reactivity
with cephalosporins in patients with severe penicillin allergy
(approx. 1–2% cross-reactivity rate).
Critical Drug Interactions:
  - Warfarin: Amoxicillin can increase INR — monitor closely.
  - Methotrexate: Reduces methotrexate renal clearance — toxicity risk.
  - Oral contraceptives: Limited evidence of reduced efficacy —
    advise additional contraception during course.
  - Allopurinol: Significantly increases risk of skin rash.
WHO Essential Medicine: Yes.

SECTION 5: ACE INHIBITORS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

ENALAPRIL (ACE Inhibitor)
Indications: Hypertension, heart failure (all stages), diabetic
nephropathy, prevention of cardiovascular events in high-risk patients,
left ventricular dysfunction post-MI.
Standard Dose:
  Hypertension: 5–40 mg once daily. Start 2.5 mg in elderly,
  sodium-depleted, or renal impairment.
  Heart failure: Start 2.5 mg twice daily. Target 10–20 mg twice daily.
Contraindications:
  - History of ACE inhibitor-induced angioedema (ABSOLUTE).
  - Bilateral renal artery stenosis.
  - Pregnancy — ALL trimesters (fetotoxic and teratogenic from
    second trimester).
  - Concurrent use with aliskiren in patients with diabetes
    or eGFR < 60 mL/min.
  - Concurrent use with sacubitril/valsartan — 36-hour washout
    required due to angioedema risk.
Critical Drug Interactions:
  - Potassium supplements / Potassium-sparing diuretics (spironolactone,
    amiloride): SEVERE hyperkalaemia risk. ACE inhibitors reduce
    aldosterone and raise serum potassium — adding exogenous potassium
    or potassium-retaining agents can cause life-threatening
    hyperkalaemia, cardiac arrhythmia, and cardiac arrest.
    Monitor potassium closely. Avoid routine co-prescription without
    documented indication and regular electrolyte monitoring.
  - NSAIDs: Triple whammy — NSAID + ACEi + diuretic associated
    with acute kidney injury. Avoid in elderly and volume-depleted.
  - Lithium: ACE inhibitors reduce lithium clearance — lithium
    toxicity risk.
Monitoring: eGFR and serum potassium at baseline, 1–2 weeks after
initiation or dose change, then every 6 months when stable.
WHO Essential Medicine: Yes.
